## What I was learning

How Redux Saga structures side effects in a React app, and how it compares to plain thunks. The goal was to feel the difference between *describing* effects (sagas) and *executing* them (thunks), and to see whether the testability payoff is real.

## Mental model

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart LR
  UI["React Component"] -->|dispatch action| Store["Redux Store"]
  Store -->|notify| Saga["Saga (generator)"]
  Saga -->|put new action| Store
  Saga -->|call API| API[("External API")]
  API -.->|response| Saga
`} />

Sagas are long-running generator functions that watch for specific actions and respond with side effects. They don't *do* the I/O directly — they *yield* effect descriptors (`call`, `put`, `take`, `select`), and the middleware executes them. That separation is what makes them easy to test.

## Patterns I worked through

- **`takeEvery` vs `takeLatest`.** Every dispatch vs cancel-in-flight. Search-as-you-type wants `takeLatest`; analytics fire-and-forget wants `takeEvery`.
- **`call(fn, args)` over `fn(args)`.** Yielding a descriptor instead of invoking directly is what makes the saga deterministic in tests.
- **`select` for store reads** instead of closing over the store object. Keeps sagas pure descriptors.
- **`race` and `cancel` for cancellation.** Real-world flows (modals, polling) need clean cancellation more often than I expected.

## Design tradeoffs

- **Sagas vs Thunks.** Sagas win on complex orchestration (cancellation, race, retry, debounce). Thunks win on simple async — the saga ceremony isn't worth it for "fetch list, set state."
- **One root saga vs many.** Splitting per feature scales better than a monolithic root saga. `all([...])` composes them.
- **Generator syntax** is the price of admission. Newcomers stumble on `yield`; familiar Promise/async-await code is more readable for simple cases.

## Lessons

- The testability claim is real — testing a saga is iterating the generator and asserting yielded values. No mocks needed.
- Sagas shine when you have *flows*, not just async calls. If every reducer slice has a single fetch action, thunks are fine.
- Debug tooling (redux-devtools-extension + saga action logging) makes a huge difference in complex apps.

## When I'd reach for it

- Multi-step workflows that need cancellation (search, multi-stage forms)
- Polling and websocket integration
- Apps with heavy cross-slice orchestration

## When I wouldn't

- Simple CRUD frontends — RTK Query or thunks are lighter
- Teams unfamiliar with generators — onboarding cost is real
